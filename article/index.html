Procedural Tokens — encoding design decisions

Most token systems already separate values from semantics. The interesting part is the connection between them. Usually it is a fixed link: one token points at another and stops there. I am interested in making that connection work more like a spreadsheet cell. It can stay static, choose one value from a set, or apply a small formula. In that sense, procedural tokens are closer to design programs than static collections: systems with internal logic that keep generating outcomes.

1. The connection is the interesting part

Assume the usual setup: raw values in one place, semantic names in another. That part is already familiar. What interests me here is not re-explaining token layers, but the kinds of links between them.

In this demo there are three useful connection types. A token can point statically to one other token. It can choose one option from a set. Or it can take an input value and do something to it. The examples below stay close to colour and spacing, but the point is the connection, not the medium.

3. Static connections

A semantic token gives one of those inputs a job in the system. In the simplest case it does that with a static connection: color.brand points at values.gray800, color.surface points at values.gray50, color.onSurface points at values.gray900.

  • color.brand = — points at values.gray800
  • color.surface = — points at values.gray50
  • color.onSurface = — paired with color.surface; the text colour that goes on it
  • color.text = — points at color.onSurface (references can chain)

Static connections are good. They are direct, inspectable, and easy to reason about. Click any ref chip and you'll see the candidates it could point at instead.

The limit shows up when the relationship matters more than the pointer. Pairs like surface / onSurface are clearly related, but a static connection still stores each side as a pinned choice. The relationship itself lives somewhere outside the token.

4. When static stops being enough

Some tokens are really decisions, not just pointers. color.buttonText = ref('values.white') stores an answer, but not the reasoning behind it: what it was compared against, why white was chosen, and when that decision should stop holding.

This is where the spreadsheet analogy helps. Instead of treating a token like a cell with a fixed link, treat it like a cell that can also hold a formula. The connection can stay static when that is enough. But when the logic matters, it can become dynamic instead.

In this demo the dynamic cases fall into two buckets. One kind of rule chooses one option from a set. The other kind takes a value and does something to it. Both are still connections between the same tokens. They are just formulas instead of fixed links.

  • They can be re-run automatically when inputs change.
  • They keep the reasoning attached to the token instead of leaving it in documentation.

The button label in the example panel already works that way. The brittle version would be color.buttonText = ref('values.white') — fine for as long as the button stays dark, wrong the moment it doesn't. Instead, it's bestContrastWith(color.brand, ramp): the rule re-runs against whatever the button background ends up being and picks the most readable step from the ramp. The token does not just name the answer. It exposes the reasoning that produced it.

Re-point the brand colour and watch the label re-decide:

color.brand =

Try pointing it at a light palette colour, then a dark one. The label flips with it — because the rule re-runs against the new background and picks the ramp step with the highest contrast. No documentation to chase down: the token is the documentation.

5. Dynamic connections in practice

A procedural token is just a token with a dynamic connection. In this demo there are two main kinds: choose one option from a set, or take a value and transform it. Both live comfortably alongside plain static refs.

The first two examples choose from a set. The next two do something to a value.

Choose one of a set: the most readable foreground

Up in section 3, color.onSurface was a plain ref to values.gray900 — a decision pinned for the light surface. It works until someone changes the surface to something dark, and then black-on-dark stops being readable.

As you scrolled into this subsection, that token was rewired in place:

color.onSurface = bestContrastWith(color.surface, values)

Same name, same place in the system — but now it carries its own reasoning. Try editing and watch the body text in the panel re-decide itself: drag the colour darker and the text flips white, drag to something mid-toned and it picks whichever entry in the values pool offers the highest contrast. The pair surface / onSurface is no longer a convention you have to maintain — it's a rule the token enforces on its own.

Paired treatment

surfaceTreatment.default = { surface, onSurface }

surface

onSurface

Treat these as one named decision, not two unrelated variables: edit the surface and the readable foreground re-decides itself to keep the pair coherent.

Choose one of a set: the faintest line that still works

Same family of rule, slightly different brief. The button's hairline outline is computed:

color.line = minContrastWith(, ramp, { ratio:  })

Where bestContrastWith picks the highest contrast available, minContrastWith picks the lowest step that still clears the threshold. The result is a soft line that's visible against the surface but never shouts. Drop the threshold and it fades; raise it and it darkens. In this demo the control snaps to the contrast levels the current ramp can actually produce, because the function is choosing from a discrete set of candidates. Flip the surface dark and it auto-inverts. The decision encoded in the token isn't "use this colour" — it's "use the faintest colour that's still readable as a boundary."

Do something to values: blend them

The seven bars in the chart aren't seven hand-picked colours. They're seven steps of a procedurally-generated ramp running between the two intents we've already named:

ramp.sN = colorMix(, , ratio)

Drag either endpoint. The chart re-tones in place because every bar reads from the same ramp, and the ramp re-runs the mix on every change. The ramp is downstream of two decisions: which surface we sit on, and which colour we use to act.

The same pattern also works at much smaller scales. A hover shade can be a rule just as easily as a palette or a spacing system; the size of the decision changes, not the idea.

6. So what

Once your tokens can hold rules, your design system gets safer to change. You stop hard-coding yesterday's answers and start preserving the logic that produced them. Change the brand colour, the surface, the base spacing, the contrast threshold — the system can recompute the dependent choices instead of asking a human to remember them.

That matters because my ambition here is not more complexity. I do not want a design system to become a dense network of clever decisions that only its author can understand. I want the system to become easier to read, easier to trust, and less intimidating to change because the reasoning is encoded directly into it, instead of being distributed across tools, habits, and tribal knowledge.

There is a practical side too. Without rules, a design system grows on a hockey stick: button.border.color, button.background.color, button.background.color.hover, on and on into the tens of thousands. With rules, hundreds of derived values compress back into a handful of inputs. The trade-off is real — darken(color.interaction, 0.15) is harder to read at a glance than color.linkHover = #133380 — but intentional: the harder-to-skim line is also the one that updates itself when its inputs move.

That shift matters for another reason: relationships matter more than individual choices. Once the system stores how values relate, not just the values themselves, it can do more than preserve consistency. It can produce discovery and surprise, surfacing combinations a human would be unlikely to pick one token at a time.

That is the narrative shift I care about. Keep the value layer and the semantic layer, but stop treating every connection between them as static. Some connections pin a choice. Others encode the rule that keeps making it. At that point tokens stop being a catalogue of settled answers and become a set of small design programs: inspectable, repeatable, and capable of surprise.

7. Beyond colour

The examples here stay close to colour and spacing because those are easy to see. But the same pattern matters even more in places where design systems still rely on convention and memory: type treatments, motion rules, icon choices, or grouped decisions like a surface/on-surface pair.

The more complex the decision, the more valuable it becomes to encode the relationship instead of pinning a result. That is where procedural tokens stop being a convenience and start becoming a real design tool: not just a way to keep systems consistent, but a way to make them more legible, more adaptable, and more capable of producing outcomes you would not have arrived at by hand.
